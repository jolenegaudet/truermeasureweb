# Qualities grid — research notes

The home page grid ("None of these receive a grade. Yet they shape almost everything
that matters.") lists twelve qualities. This file records the evidence behind each word,
so future revisions keep the claim defensible. Last revised: 2026-07-09.

Standard: every quality must (a) have a credible research base as a childhood construct
linked to meaningful outcomes, and (b) not appear as a graded item on common report
cards (e.g., Ontario's graded Learning Skills: responsibility, organization, independent
work, collaboration, initiative, self-regulation — see caveats below).

## The twelve

**01 Confidence** — best grounded as *self-efficacy* (Bandura). Self-efficacy beliefs
predict academic performance and persistence across many studies. Evidence: strong,
though "confidence" is the lay translation, not the measured construct.

**02 Curiosity** — epistemic curiosity is linked to academic achievement roughly on par
with effort (von Stumm, Hell & Chamorro-Premuzic, 2011, "The Hungry Mind"). Evidence:
strong.

**03 Courage** — thinnest direct evidence base of the twelve as a measured childhood
construct; retained for its emotional truth and its overlap with well-studied
approach-vs-avoidance and risk-taking literatures. Treat as values language, not a
research claim.

**04 Imagination** — grounded in pretend-play and counterfactual-reasoning research
(Gopnik; Weisberg). Evidence linking early imaginative play to later creativity is
suggestive rather than settled (Lillard et al., 2013 review urges caution). Replaced
*Initiative* on 2026-07-09 because initiative is a letter-graded Learning Skill on
Ontario report cards, which contradicted the section heading.

**05 Resilience** — decades of developmental work; Masten's "ordinary magic" framing
(Masten, 2001): resilience arises from ordinary adaptive systems (attachment, schools,
self-regulation), not rare heroism. Evidence: strong.

**06 Creativity** — established field with validated measures (divergent thinking,
Torrance tests); childhood creativity scores predict adult creative achievement in
longitudinal follow-ups. Evidence: strong.

**07 Self-Regulation** — the strongest single entry. Dunedin cohort (~1,000 children
followed 30+ years): childhood self-control predicted adult health, wealth, and criminal
outcomes independent of IQ and family income (Moffitt et al., 2011, PNAS). Related:
executive function research (Diamond, 2013). Evidence: very strong.
Caveat: "self-regulation" is also a graded Learning Skill in Ontario; we accept the
overlap because the underlying construct is too important to omit. If the heading claim
is ever challenged, this is the word to defend or reword first.

**08 Persistence** — deliberately worded as persistence, not "grit." Conscientiousness
rivals intelligence as a predictor of academic performance (Poropat, 2009 meta-analysis).
Grit itself largely duplicates conscientiousness and shows weak incremental validity
(Credé, Tynan & Harms, 2017 meta-analysis). Evidence: strong for the construct; avoid
the grit branding.

**09 Empathy** — kindergarten prosocial skills predicted education, employment, substance
use, and mental-health outcomes ~20 years later (Jones, Greenberg & Crowley, 2015,
Am J Public Health). SEL program meta-analysis shows broad benefits (Durlak et al., 2011).
Evidence: strong.

**10 Adaptability** — grounded in cognitive flexibility, a core executive function tied
to school readiness and long-run outcomes (Diamond, 2013). Also appears in OECD/Education
2030 frameworks, which is positioning, not evidence. Evidence: moderate-to-strong.

**11 Hope** — Snyder's hope theory (agency + pathways thinking). Hope scores predict
academic achievement beyond intelligence in several studies (Snyder, 2002; Day et al.,
2010). Evidence: moderate; the construct is well defined but the literature is smaller.

**12 Purpose** — Damon's Stanford work on youth purpose; purposeful adolescents show
higher motivation and wellbeing. Evidence: directional and largely correlational —
credible for a "matters in life" claim, not a causal one.

## Deliberately excluded

- **Grit** — brand tied to one researcher; meta-analytic overlap with conscientiousness
  (Credé et al., 2017). "Persistence" carries the substance without the baggage.
- **Growth Mindset** — real but small effects in large-scale replications (Yeager et al.,
  2019 national study: modest gains, concentrated in lower-achieving students). Too
  contested for a flagship claim.
- **Gratitude / Mindfulness** — child-outcome evidence thinner; reads as wellness content.
- **Leadership** — not a stable, measurable childhood construct.
- **Initiative** — removed 2026-07-09; letter-graded on Ontario report cards.
